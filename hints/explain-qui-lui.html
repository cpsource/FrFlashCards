Explaining qui lui

We start from the French sentence:

Il a une corne qui lui sort de la tête.

He has a horn that comes out of his head.

1. What does qui do?

In this sentence, qui means that or which, and it is the subject of the verb sort (comes out).

So we can imagine two simple sentences:

Il a une corne.
Une corne sort de la tête.

When we join them, we get:

Il a une corne qui sort de la tête.

Here, qui replaces une corne and becomes the subject of sort. It answers the question: What is coming out of the head? → The horn.

2. What does lui do?

Now, why do we say qui lui sort de la tête and not just qui sort de la tête?

The word lui means to him or to her. It is an indirect object pronoun. It tells us that the horn comes out of his head.

More explicitly, we could think of:

La corne sort de la tête de lui.

In good French, we do not say de lui after la tête. Instead, we put lui before the verb:

La corne lui sort de la tête.

So the full sentence becomes:

Il a une corne qui lui sort de la tête.

Literally: “He has a horn that to-him comes out of the head.” In normal English: He has a horn that comes out of his head.

3. Another example: Elle a une idée qui lui vient à l’esprit

The same pattern appears in this sentence:

Elle a une idée qui lui vient à l’esprit.

She has an idea that comes to her mind.

Again, we can imagine two simple sentences:

Elle a une idée.
Une idée vient à son esprit.

When we join them, we get:

Elle a une idée qui vient à son esprit.

But in French, with a person, we often use the pronoun lui:

Elle a une idée qui lui vient à l’esprit.

Here:

  • qui = subject of vient (it replaces une idée).
  • lui = to her (the idea comes to her).

4. More examples with the same pattern

  • Example 1
    Il a une chanson qui lui reste dans la tête. He has a song that stays in his head (a song stuck in his head).

    qui = subject of reste (the song stays).
    lui = to him (in his head).

  • Example 2
    J’ai une douleur qui me vient dans le dos. I have a pain that comes into my back.

    Same pattern, but with me instead of lui. The pain comes to me, into my back.

  • Example 3
    Elle a une idée qui lui passe par la tête. She has an idea that passes through her mind.

    qui still refers to l’idée (subject of passe), and lui means “to her”.

5. Summary

In sentences like:

Il a une corne qui lui sort de la tête.
Elle a une idée qui lui vient à l’esprit.

  • qui is a relative pronoun that replaces a noun and becomes the subject of the following verb.
  • lui is an indirect object pronoun (to him / to her) and often appears with expressions like:
    • lui venir à l’esprit – to come to his/her mind
    • lui sortir de la tête – to come out of his/her head
    • lui rester dans la tête – to stay in his/her head

So, qui lui is not one single word. It’s really:
“qui” (subject of the verb) + “lui” (to him / to her), working together to describe what happens to the person.
