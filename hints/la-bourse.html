La Bourse – What Does It Mean?

Can la Bourse be mistaken for the stock exchange? Yes – and most of the time, that’s exactly what it means. But context matters, because bourse has a few different meanings.

1. La Bourse = the stock exchange / the market most common

With a capital B, la Bourse almost always means the stock exchange or the stock market.

  • La Bourse de Paris a beaucoup baissé aujourd’hui.
    → The Paris stock exchange fell sharply today.
  • Investir en Bourse peut être risqué.
    → Investing in the stock market can be risky.
  • Les actions montent à la Bourse de New York.
    → Stocks are rising on the New York exchange.

So yes: when you see la Bourse in a financial context, it’s referring to the stock exchange.

2. Une bourse = a scholarship or grant

In school or university contexts, une bourse usually means a scholarship, grant, or financial aid.

  • Il a obtenu une bourse d’études.
    → He got a scholarship.
  • Les étudiants étrangers peuvent demander une bourse.
    → Foreign students can apply for a grant.

Here, context (studies, university, students) makes it clear that bourse is not the stock market.

3. Une bourse = a purse or pouch (older / literary use)

Historically, une bourse was a small money bag or purse. You’ll see this more in literature, stories, or fixed expressions.

  • Il a sorti quelques pièces de sa bourse.
    → He took a few coins from his purse.
  • Avoir la bourse plate
    → To be broke (literally: to have a flat purse).

4. Summary: how to know which meaning?

  • La Bourse (capital B, finance context) → the stock exchange / stock market.
  • une bourse (school / studies context) → a scholarship or grant.
  • une bourse (story / old-fashioned context) → a purse or money pouch.

So yes, la Bourse can be “mistaken” for the stock exchange – but in most real-life situations, that’s exactly what it is. The key is the capital letter and the context.
